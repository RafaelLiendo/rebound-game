# Plan: Permeation Buoyancy Game Feel

## Intent

Create a game about passing through matter, surrendering control for a moment,
and using the pressure of the world to come back with momentum.

The central fantasy is not simply becoming intangible. It is learning to treat
solid space as a temporary medium: something the player can enter, read, and use
to redirect motion. The mechanic should feel strange at first, then expressive
once the player understands its rhythm.

## Core Experience

The player moves through a platforming space where some obstacles are not only
barriers, but opportunities. A wall, floor, or block can become a place to dive,
wait, and emerge with force.

The game should make the player feel:

- light and agile while moving normally
- slowed and suspended while inside solid matter
- tense when deciding when to reappear
- powerful when a well-timed release turns depth into upward motion
- clever when a puzzle is solved by using terrain as a launch medium

## Movement Feel

Basic movement should feel responsive, readable, and generous enough that the
special mechanic can be the focus. The player should trust ordinary movement
before being asked to bend the rules of collision.

Jumping and aerial motion should support expressive correction. The player
should be able to commit, adjust, and recover without the game feeling loose or
automatic.

Normal movement is the baseline language. Permeation is the accent.

## Permeation

Permeation lets the player enter solid matter instead of colliding with it.
While permeating, the player should feel less like they are falling freely and
more like they are moving through a resistant medium.

The sensation should be:

- muted
- pressurized
- slightly uncanny
- readable enough for timing
- slower than ordinary falling through open air

The player should understand that entering matter changes the rules of motion.
It should not feel like a simple invisibility or noclip state. Matter should
have presence.

## Buoyant Rebound

Rebound is the release of stored pressure. When the player exits the medium at
the right moment, the terrain pushes them out with rising force.

A good rebound should feel earned. The player should sense that the launch comes
from having been inside the matter, not from pressing a generic jump button.

The force should feel like buoyancy rather than an explosion:

- motion begins from compression
- speed gathers while emerging
- the strongest moment happens near escape
- the player regains familiar control after surfacing

Depth should matter as a player concept. Going deeper should feel more charged,
more committed, and more rewarding, even if the exact relationship is tuned
through playtesting later.

## Timing And Intention

The mechanic should reward intent rather than precision alone. The player should
be asked questions like:

- Do I enter here or keep moving?
- Do I release now or sink a little farther?
- Am I using this block to cross, climb, escape, or chain into another move?
- Is this terrain a threat, a tool, or both?

The best moments should feel like discovering a hidden verb inside the level.

## Puzzle Shape

Levels should teach the mechanic through situations that naturally invite
experimentation.

Early spaces should make the player comfortable with entering and exiting
matter. Later spaces can ask the player to combine ordinary movement,
permeation, rebound, drift, and timing.

Puzzle ideas:

- passing through a floor to reach a lower route
- diving into thick terrain to launch upward
- choosing where inside a mass to release
- using rebound height to reach offset platforms
- chaining permeation and rebound across multiple surfaces
- avoiding reappearing in places that trap or punish poor timing

Each puzzle should have a clear physical idea. The player should be able to
look at the space and form a hypothesis before executing it.

## Risk And Recovery

The mechanic can create danger when the player misreads the terrain or releases
at the wrong time. That danger should feel fair and legible.

Failure states should communicate the cause:

- the player surfaced too early
- the player released in the wrong part of the mass
- the player became trapped inside matter
- the player lacked enough rebound energy for the intended route

Recovery should be quick. The game should encourage repeated attempts and
experimentation rather than punishing curiosity.

## Feedback

The player needs strong sensory feedback for state changes.

Permeation should have a distinct visual and audio identity from ordinary
movement. Rebound should feel like a buildup and release. Failed or unsafe
states should be unmistakable without becoming noisy.

Feedback should clarify:

- whether the player is solid or permeating
- whether the player is inside matter
- whether pressure is building
- when rebound begins
- when control returns fully
- when the player is trapped or unsafe

## Feel Pillars

- **Matter has weight.** Solid terrain should feel like a physical medium, not
  a disabled collision layer.
- **Depth creates commitment.** Going farther in should feel meaningful and a
  little risky.
- **Release is expressive.** The key decision is when and where to emerge.
- **Momentum is a reward.** Good timing turns constraint into movement.
- **Levels teach through shape.** The environment should suggest experiments
  before demanding mastery.
- **Failure stays playful.** Mistakes should invite another try, not frustration.

## Open Design Questions

- How much control should the player retain while inside matter?
- Should rebound always express upward motion, or can different terrain imply
  different exits?
- Should chaining be an advanced skill, an assisted mode, or part of the core
  verb?
- How should the game distinguish safe permeation from dangerous entrapment?
- What kinds of enemies, hazards, or moving objects make the mechanic richer
  without confusing its physical language?
